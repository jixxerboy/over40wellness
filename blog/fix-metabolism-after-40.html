How to Fix Your Metabolism After 40 (Without Going on a Diet)

22 June 2026  ·  4 min read

← Back to Blog

"My metabolism has slowed down" is one of the most common things people say about their 40s — and it's partly true, but also significantly misunderstood. Yes, metabolic rate does decline with age. But research suggests the actual slowdown from pure ageing is much smaller than people assume — only around 1–2% per decade between 20 and 60.

The bigger culprit is muscle loss. Muscle tissue burns significantly more calories at rest than fat tissue does. As muscle mass declines — often invisibly, without any change in scale weight — your metabolic rate drops with it. And the good news about muscle loss is that it's largely preventable and reversible.

Build and Protect Muscle Mass

Resistance training — lifting weights, using resistance bands, bodyweight exercises — is the most direct way to reverse age-related muscle loss and restore a flagging metabolism. Even two sessions a week of 30–40 minutes produces measurable improvements in muscle mass within 8–12 weeks.

You don't need a gym. A set of resistance bands and a few bodyweight movements (squats, push-ups, rows, lunges) done consistently will produce significant results. The key is progressive overload — gradually making the exercises harder over time so your muscles continue to adapt. Doing the same workout at the same intensity indefinitely produces no new benefit.

Eat More Protein Than You Think You Need

Protein has the highest thermic effect of any macronutrient — digesting it burns roughly 25–30% of its calories, compared to 5–10% for carbohydrates. Eating more protein genuinely boosts your metabolic rate slightly. More importantly, adequate protein (1.6g per kg bodyweight) is essential for maintaining and building the muscle mass that drives your resting metabolism.

Most people in their 40s are significantly under-eating protein and don't realise it. Track your intake honestly for three days and see where you land. Prioritise protein at every meal: eggs or Greek yoghurt at breakfast, a palm-sized portion of meat, fish, or legumes at lunch and dinner.

Don't Cut Calories Aggressively

This is the counterintuitive one. Severe calorie restriction signals your body to downregulate metabolism to match the reduced energy intake — a survival mechanism that worked brilliantly in times of famine and works against you when you're trying to lose weight in the modern world. Restrictive dieting also accelerates muscle loss, which compounds the metabolic slowdown.

The approach that works long-term: a modest calorie deficit (300–500 calories below your needs) combined with high protein and resistance training. You lose fat while preserving muscle, and your metabolism doesn't crater in response. Slow, steady progress beats repeated cycles of aggressive restriction and rebound.

Move More Throughout the Day, Not Just at the Gym

NEAT — Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis — is the energy you burn through daily movement that isn't formal exercise: walking to the shops, taking the stairs, fidgeting, cooking, gardening. NEAT varies enormously between individuals and can account for several hundred extra calories burned per day for active people versus sedentary ones.

After 40, when sedentary work becomes more common and formal exercise often becomes less frequent, NEAT tends to collapse. Getting a step counter and targeting 8,000–10,000 steps per day (most of which come from daily life rather than dedicated walks) is a practical way to boost NEAT meaningfully. Even standing at your desk for two hours instead of sitting makes a measurable difference.

Sleep Is a Metabolic Issue

Poor sleep directly impairs metabolism in several ways: it increases ghrelin (the hunger hormone), reduces leptin (the satiety hormone), and drives cravings for high-calorie foods. One week of sleeping less than six hours increases insulin resistance measurably. Over months and years, poor sleep is a genuine driver of weight gain and metabolic deterioration.

If you're working on your metabolism and not sleeping well, fix the sleep first. The dietary and exercise interventions work dramatically better on a foundation of adequate sleep.

The Bottom Line

Your metabolism after 40 responds to the same inputs as it always did — it just requires more intentionality. Build muscle, eat enough protein, keep moving throughout the day, sleep well, and avoid the crash-diet cycle. None of these require a radical overhaul — just a consistent, patient approach over six months or more.
