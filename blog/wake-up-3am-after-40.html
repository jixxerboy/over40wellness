Why You Wake Up at 3am and Can't Get Back to Sleep

22 June 2026  ·  4 min read

← Back to Blog

It's one of the most consistent patterns I hear from people in their 40s: falling asleep is fine, but somewhere between 2am and 4am, they're wide awake. Mind racing, unable to get comfortable, lying there watching the minutes tick by until they finally drift off just before the alarm goes.

This is so common it almost seems normal — but it isn't. It has specific causes, and once you know them, you can address them directly.

The Blood Sugar Drop Mechanism

The most common cause of the 3am wake-up is a blood sugar crash. Here's what happens: you eat dinner, blood sugar rises, insulin is released to bring it back down. If you've eaten a carbohydrate-heavy dinner without enough protein or fat to slow absorption, blood sugar can drop too low in the early hours.

When blood sugar gets too low, your body triggers a cortisol release to raise it — and cortisol is alerting. It wakes you up. This is a survival mechanism, not a malfunction. But if it's happening nightly, it means your evening eating pattern needs adjusting.

The fix: eat a small protein-rich snack before bed. A tablespoon of nut butter, a small handful of almonds, or some full-fat Greek yoghurt. These provide slow-release fuel that prevents the overnight drop. Try this consistently for a week and see if your 3am waking reduces.

Alcohol Is Almost Certainly Involved

If you have a drink in the evening, alcohol is likely contributing to your 3am problem — even if it initially helps you fall asleep. Alcohol is metabolised to acetaldehyde, a stimulant, in the early hours. It also suppresses REM sleep in the first half of the night, then causes rebound REM in the second half, which is lighter and more easily disrupted.

Even one or two drinks have measurable effects on sleep quality in the second half of the night. I'm not saying don't drink — but if you're struggling with early-morning waking, try cutting alcohol for two weeks and see what happens. Most people are surprised by how dramatically their 3am waking reduces.

Cortisol Naturally Peaks in the Early Morning

Cortisol follows a daily rhythm, with its natural peak occurring between 6am and 8am. After 40, this peak can shift earlier — and in people with higher chronic stress, cortisol levels in the pre-dawn hours can be high enough to cause arousal. This is why waking at 3–4am often comes with a racing mind and a sense of low-level anxiety: it's cortisol doing its job, just a few hours too early.

The long-term fix here is managing overall stress load. The shorter-term fix: don't fight it with a scrolling session on your phone (which will make things dramatically worse). Instead, keep the room dark, try slow 4-7-8 breathing (inhale 4 counts, hold 7, exhale 8), and accept that you may lie quietly for 20–30 minutes before drifting off. Resistance and clock-watching make it worse.

Temperature Is a Common Culprit

Deep sleep requires your core body temperature to drop. In the early morning hours, as your sleep naturally lightens, you're more sensitive to temperature disruption. Being too warm — from a partner, heavy bedding, or a warm room — can pull you out of sleep in the 2–4am window.

Try sleeping with slightly lighter bedding than feels comfortable when you get in. You'll warm up quickly, and in the vulnerable early-morning window, you'll be less likely to overheat. A cool bedroom (16–18°C) and a window cracked open makes a noticeable difference for most people.

When to Actually Worry

Occasional 3am waking is normal. Persistent waking most nights, especially if it's accompanied by significant anxiety, heart pounding, or has been going on for months, is worth mentioning to your GP. Sleep apnoea — which becomes more common in your 40s and 50s — can cause frequent night wakings and is both serious and very treatable.

But for the majority of people reading this, the blood sugar and alcohol factors are the place to start. Both are testable in a week. Make the changes, observe the results, and go from there.
