Your Gut Is Ageing — And It's Affecting Everything Else

22 June 2026  ·  5 min read

← Back to Blog

If you've been wondering why your digestion feels different than it did ten years ago, or why you seem to catch every bug going round, or why your mood and mental sharpness aren't quite what they were — part of the answer may be happening in your gut. And the research on this is moving fast.

The gut microbiome — the trillions of bacteria, fungi, and other microorganisms living in your digestive tract — changes significantly with age. Recent research has made the connections between these changes and virtually every aspect of health over 40 much clearer. Here's what we now know, and what you can actually do about it.

What Changes in the Gut After 40

Diversity is the key word in gut health research. A healthy gut has a large variety of different bacterial species, each doing different jobs: digesting fibre, producing vitamins, training the immune system, communicating with the brain. After 40, and accelerating through the 50s, this diversity tends to decline. Beneficial bacteria like Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus decrease. Inflammatory bacterial species fill the gap.

The result is what researchers call "inflammaging" — a low-grade, chronic inflammatory state that appears to drive much of what we associate with ageing: joint pain, cognitive decline, cardiovascular risk, metabolic slowdown, and impaired immune response. It's not dramatic. You don't feel it acutely. But it compounds over years.

Recent research: A 2025 large-scale study ranked gut bacteria by their associations with health outcomes and diet, finding that specific beneficial species were strongly linked to lower rates of type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease — and that these species are predominantly fed by dietary fibre.

The Gut-Brain Connection Is Real and Bidirectional

One of the most significant findings of recent years is just how directly the gut and brain communicate. The gut-brain axis — a two-way signalling network via the vagus nerve, immune system, and gut-produced neurotransmitters — means that what happens in your gut directly affects your mood, focus, anxiety levels, and cognitive function.

Around 90% of your body's serotonin is produced in the gut, not the brain. Certain gut bacteria produce short-chain fatty acids that cross the blood-brain barrier and reduce neuroinflammation. Research hotspots in 2025 include microglia (the brain's immune cells, which are modulated by gut bacteria) and the intestinal barrier — when the gut lining becomes "leaky," bacterial toxins enter the bloodstream and drive systemic inflammation that affects the brain.

This is why gut health is increasingly discussed alongside brain fog, low mood, and anxiety — not as a metaphor, but as a direct mechanistic link.

Gut Health and Cardiovascular Risk

2025 research has clarified how specific gut microbial metabolites promote plaque build-up in arteries — a process that was previously attributed mainly to diet and genetics. Certain bacteria produce TMAO (trimethylamine N-oxide) from red meat and eggs, which directly contributes to arterial plaque. Others adjust bile acid metabolism in ways that affect cholesterol balance.

This doesn't mean avoiding red meat entirely. But it does mean that the bacterial composition of your gut affects how any food affects your cardiovascular system — which is why two people can eat the same diet and have very different metabolic outcomes.

What Actually Improves Gut Diversity After 40

The encouraging news: the gut microbiome is one of the most responsive systems in the body. It can shift meaningfully within days of changing your diet. Here's what the research supports most clearly:

  • Eat more fibre, and more varied fibre — 30 different plant foods per week is the target cited in the American Gut Project research. This doesn't mean 30 different meals — herbs, spices, nuts, seeds, and different coloured vegetables all count. Variety of plant fibre is more important than quantity alone.
  • Fermented foods daily — A landmark 2021 Stanford study (still frequently cited) found that a high-fermented-food diet increased microbiome diversity and reduced inflammatory markers more effectively than a high-fibre diet alone. Yoghurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi, kombucha — one or two servings a day matters.
  • Reduce ultra-processed food — emulsifiers, artificial sweeteners, and food additives have been shown to damage the gut lining and reduce microbial diversity. This is a significant mechanism by which processed food harms health beyond just its caloric content.
  • Exercise regularly — physical activity directly increases gut microbial diversity, independent of diet. Even moderate activity like regular walking produces measurable improvements in microbiome composition over 8–12 weeks.
  • Manage stress — chronic stress alters gut motility and bacterial composition through the gut-brain axis. The relationship goes both ways: a healthier gut makes stress easier to manage, but you need to reduce stress to improve gut health.

What About Probiotic Supplements?

Probiotic supplements are popular but the evidence is more nuanced than their marketing suggests. Generic multi-strain probiotics have inconsistent evidence for general gut health improvement — primarily because strains that survive transit and colonise effectively vary enormously between individuals. Where probiotics have stronger evidence is in specific contexts: after a course of antibiotics, for specific conditions like IBS or certain infections, and for targeted strains at therapeutic doses.

For general gut health after 40, the dietary and lifestyle interventions above have significantly stronger and more consistent evidence than supplementation. Fermented foods appear to outperform probiotic supplements in most comparative studies — primarily because they provide a wider range of live organisms in a more bioavailable form.

The Bottom Line

Gut health after 40 is increasingly recognised as a central pillar of overall wellbeing — not just digestion, but energy, mood, cognitive function, immune resilience, and long-term disease risk. The interventions aren't complicated: more varied plants, regular fermented foods, less ultra-processed food, exercise, and stress management. What makes them powerful is that they work across multiple systems simultaneously.
