Why Your Energy Crashed After 40 (And How to Get It Back)

22 June 2026  ·  4 min read

← Back to Blog

There's a specific kind of tiredness that arrives in your 40s that feels different from being busy or stressed. It's a baseline fatigue — a flat battery that coffee can't fully charge, and that a good night's sleep doesn't completely fix. If you recognise that feeling, you're not alone, and you're not imagining it.

The causes are real, biological, and — crucially — addressable. Here's what's actually happening and what you can do about it.

Your Mitochondria Are Slowing Down

Mitochondria are the energy-producing structures inside your cells. After 40, their efficiency declines — they produce less ATP (your body's energy currency) from the same amount of food. This is one reason why you can eat similarly to how you did at 30 and still feel like you're running on empty.

What helps: regular moderate exercise — particularly zone 2 cardio (a pace where you can hold a conversation) — stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis, meaning your body makes more mitochondria. Even 30 minutes of brisk walking four days a week measurably improves cellular energy production over 8–12 weeks.

Hormonal Changes Hit Your Energy Hard

Testosterone (in men) and oestrogen (in women) both decline through your 40s. Both hormones play a direct role in how your body produces and uses energy. Lower testosterone reduces muscle mass and motivation. Lower oestrogen disrupts sleep quality and thyroid function. Either way, the result is fatigue that feels disproportionate to what you've actually done.

If your tiredness is severe and persistent, it's worth getting a blood panel done — thyroid function, testosterone, and iron/ferritin are the key ones to check. Many people in their 40s find their thyroid has quietly slowed down, which is entirely treatable once identified.

Blood Sugar Swings Are Amplified After 40

Your insulin sensitivity naturally decreases with age, which means blood sugar spikes and crashes become more dramatic. That post-lunch slump you've started noticing? That's a blood sugar crash, and it tends to get worse in your 40s. The fix isn't willpower — it's restructuring your meals.

Eat protein and fat before carbohydrates at each meal. This simple sequencing change blunts the glucose spike significantly. A handful of nuts before a carb-heavy meal, or starting your plate with the meat or fish, can prevent the crash that wipes you out at 2pm.

You're Probably Not Getting Enough Protein

After 40, your body becomes less efficient at using dietary protein to repair and maintain muscle. The result is slow muscle loss (sarcopenia) that compounds year on year — and muscle tissue is metabolically active, meaning less of it means lower baseline energy. Most people over 40 are significantly under-eating protein.

The evidence-backed target is roughly 1.6g of protein per kilogram of bodyweight per day. For a 75kg person, that's 120g of protein — more than most people eat. Prioritise protein at breakfast (eggs, Greek yoghurt, smoked salmon) and it becomes much easier to hit the target by the end of the day.

Dehydration Is Silently Draining You

Even mild dehydration — as little as 1–2% of body weight — causes measurable fatigue, reduced concentration, and a low mood. After 40, your thirst mechanism becomes less reliable; you can be meaningfully dehydrated without feeling particularly thirsty.

I found the simplest fix was to drink 500ml of water before coffee in the morning and to keep a 1-litre bottle on my desk during work hours. Within a week, my afternoon energy was noticeably better — and I hadn't changed anything else.

The Role of Sleep (Again)

Poor sleep and low energy form a vicious cycle after 40. Low energy leads to less motivation to wind down properly, which leads to poor sleep, which leads to lower energy. Breaking the cycle requires tackling both together rather than hoping one fixes the other.

If you're working on your energy and not seeing results, go back to basics on sleep: consistent bedtimes, a cool dark room, and no alcohol within three hours of bed. These are not exciting advice, but they work in ways that no supplement can replicate.

Where to Start

  • Check your protein intake — track it honestly for three days and see where you land
  • Add a 30-minute walk four days a week — zone 2 cardio for mitochondria
  • Restructure your meals — protein and fat before carbs, every meal
  • Drink water before coffee — make it a non-negotiable morning habit

Energy after 40 responds surprisingly well to the basics done consistently. Give it six weeks before chasing any supplement or intervention beyond these.
