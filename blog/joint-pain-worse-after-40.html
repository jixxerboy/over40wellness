Joint Health

5 Things Making Your Joint Pain Worse After 40

22 June 2026  ·  4 min read

← Back to Blog

Achy knees getting out of a car. Stiff hips in the morning. That shoulder that clicks every time you reach up. If your joints have started making themselves known in your 40s, welcome to a club nobody asked to join.

Here's the thing, though: most joint pain at this age isn't just "getting old." It's lifestyle factors — and several of them are surprisingly fixable. Here are the five most common ones I see people making, often without realising it.

1. Sitting for Long Stretches Without Moving

Joints are lubricated by synovial fluid, which gets pumped around the joint through movement. When you sit still for hours, that fluid stagnates, cartilage dries out slightly, and the surrounding muscles stiffen — which puts more load on the joint when you do finally move. This is why you feel worse standing up from a desk than you did when you sat down.

The fix is simple: stand up and move for two minutes every 40–50 minutes. Not a full workout — just walk to the kitchen, do some hip circles, climb the stairs. Your joints need circulation, not rest.

2. Skipping the Warm-Up

In your 20s, you could go from cold to full effort without much consequence. After 40, tissue elasticity decreases and connective tissue takes longer to warm up — jumping straight into exercise on cold joints dramatically increases the risk of minor tears and inflammation that accumulate over time.

Five minutes of dynamic movement before any exercise pays back tenfold. Not static stretching (which is actually contraindicated when muscles are cold) but movement-based warm-up: leg swings, arm circles, gentle squats that take joints through their range of motion. Make it non-negotiable.

3. Chronic Low-Grade Dehydration

Cartilage is approximately 70% water. When you're chronically under-hydrated, cartilage becomes less resilient, absorbs impact less effectively, and degrades faster. Most people over 40 are mildly dehydrated throughout the day — not enough to feel thirsty, but enough to affect joint health over months and years.

Target around 2.5 litres of water per day for most adults. Coffee and tea count, but alcohol doesn't — it actively dehydrates you. The simplest habit: a 500ml glass of water first thing in the morning before anything else.

4. Too Many Pro-Inflammatory Foods

Certain foods actively drive inflammation throughout the body, including in joint tissue. The main culprits: refined sugar, seed oils (vegetable oil, sunflower oil), ultra-processed foods, and excess alcohol. These raise systemic inflammatory markers that make existing joint pain worse and accelerate cartilage breakdown.

You don't need a restrictive diet — just a consistent tilt towards anti-inflammatory foods: oily fish (salmon, mackerel), olive oil, leafy greens, berries, nuts, and turmeric. These aren't cures, but the cumulative effect of eating this way over months is real. Several of my readers have reduced their morning stiffness significantly just through dietary changes.

5. Avoiding Movement Because of the Pain

This one's counterintuitive but important. When joints hurt, the natural instinct is to rest them. For acute injuries, that's right. But for chronic joint discomfort — the kind that's been building for months or years — rest usually makes things worse. Muscles weaken, joints destabilise further, and pain increases.

The evidence is clear: low-impact movement is medicine for arthritic and degenerative joint conditions. Swimming, cycling, walking, and resistance training all reduce joint pain over time by strengthening the supporting muscles, improving lubrication, and reducing systemic inflammation. Start gentle, stay consistent, and don't use a bad day as a reason to stop entirely.

The One Change to Make First

If you're overwhelmed by this list, start with hydration and movement breaks. Drink more water and interrupt your sitting every hour. These two changes cost nothing, require no new routine, and most people notice a difference within two weeks. Build from there.
