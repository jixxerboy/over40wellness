Why Walking Is the Best Thing You Can Do for Your Joints After 40

22 June 2026  ·  4 min read

← Back to Blog

When joints start aching, the advice most people receive is to rest them. Take it easy. Don't overdo it. This advice, while well-intentioned, often makes joint problems worse — and it keeps people stuck in a cycle of stiffness, weakness, and increasing pain.

The counterintuitive truth: movement is medicine for joints. And of all the forms of movement available to people over 40, walking consistently delivers the best results for joint health with the lowest risk of making things worse. Here's why — and how to do it right.

Walking Lubricates Your Joints

Synovial fluid — the lubricant inside your joints — doesn't flow freely when you're stationary. It gets pumped around the joint through movement, coating cartilage and reducing friction. When you sit still for hours, the fluid stagnates, cartilage becomes less protected, and joints feel stiffer when you finally do move.

Regular walking keeps the pump working. Even a 20-minute walk distributes synovial fluid through your knees, hips, and ankles, reducing that morning stiffness and that "first few steps" pain that so many people over 40 recognise.

It Strengthens the Muscles That Protect Your Joints

Joints don't work in isolation — they depend on the surrounding muscles to share the load. The quadriceps protect the knee. The glutes and hip flexors protect the hip. The core protects the lumbar spine. When these muscles are weak, all the force of movement goes directly into the joint, accelerating wear and increasing pain.

Walking consistently strengthens all of these supporting muscle groups — not dramatically, but steadily, over months. This is why the research on walking for osteoarthritis is so consistently positive: the benefit isn't just lubrication, it's structural support building up quietly over time.

It Reduces Systemic Inflammation

Chronic low-grade inflammation is at the root of most degenerative joint conditions. After 40, inflammatory markers tend to creep upward — driven by excess body weight, poor diet, stress, and sedentary behaviour. This inflammation directly accelerates cartilage breakdown in affected joints.

Regular moderate exercise — including brisk walking — is one of the most reliably anti-inflammatory interventions available. It reduces C-reactive protein and other inflammatory markers measurably, with effects that appear within weeks of starting a consistent routine. No supplement does this as reliably or as safely.

It's Low-Impact Enough to Start When You're Hurting

One of walking's key advantages is that it can be started even when joints are painful, because the impact forces are much lower than running or high-intensity exercise. You can calibrate the intensity and duration to your current condition and gradually build from there.

If even flat walking is painful, start with shorter distances (10 minutes) or find slightly softer surfaces (grass, trails rather than pavement). The goal in the first weeks isn't fitness — it's rebuilding the habit of movement and letting the joint-lubrication benefits accumulate.

How to Do It Right

A few practical points that make a significant difference:

  • Warm up first — three minutes of slow walking before you hit your pace. Cold joints need easing in.
  • Focus on posture — walk tall, shoulders back, core slightly engaged. Slouched walking puts more load on your lower back and hips.
  • Wear supportive footwear — not necessarily expensive, but with decent cushioning and arch support. Worn-out trainers or flat shoes undermine every benefit.
  • Go after dinner — a 20-minute post-dinner walk lowers blood sugar, reduces evening cortisol, and improves sleep. Three benefits in one walk.
  • Aim for 30 minutes, 5 days a week — this is the evidence-backed threshold for meaningful joint and inflammatory benefits.

What If Walking Is Painful Right Now?

If a specific joint is acutely inflamed or injured, walking may not be appropriate until the acute phase has settled — see your GP or physiotherapist for anything that's significantly worsened recently. But for the chronic, low-level joint ache that characterises most over-40 joint complaints, gentle walking is almost always the right medicine.

Start where you are. Even five minutes is better than none. Build by no more than 10% per week. And give it six weeks before you judge whether it's working — joint health improvements are real but gradual.
