Why You Can't Sleep After 40 (And What Actually Helps)

22 June 2026  ·  4 min read

← Back to Blog

If you've hit your 40s and suddenly find yourself lying awake at midnight, or waking at 3am with a brain that refuses to switch off, you're not imagining it. Sleep genuinely changes after 40 — and the frustrating part is that nobody warned you it would.

I spent two years thinking I just had anxiety, or that I was drinking too much coffee, or that stress was the culprit. All of those played a role. But the root cause was biological, and once I understood what was actually happening, I could finally do something about it.

Your Sleep Architecture Shifts With Age

When you were younger, you likely dropped quickly into deep, restorative sleep. After 40, the proportion of that deep sleep (called slow-wave sleep) starts to shrink. You get more light sleep, you wake more easily, and you spend less time in the genuinely restorative stages. This isn't a disorder — it's a normal change. But it does mean you can't just fall asleep and expect your body to do the rest.

Melatonin Production Declines

Your pineal gland produces less melatonin as you age. Melatonin is the hormone that signals to your body that it's time to sleep — it doesn't knock you out like a drug, but it acts as a timing cue for your circadian rhythm. Less of it means a weaker signal. The knock-on effect: you feel less sleepy at bedtime, you take longer to fall asleep, and you're more sensitive to light disrupting your cycle.

Practical fix: dim every light in your home after 8pm. Blue light from screens is the biggest offender — it suppresses melatonin production more than any other wavelength. Night mode on your phone isn't enough; the brightness itself is the problem.

Cortisol Takes Longer to Drop in the Evening

Cortisol — your primary stress hormone — follows a natural daily curve: high in the morning, gradually dropping through the day, lowest at night. After 40, this curve can flatten. Cortisol stays elevated longer in the evening, keeping your nervous system alert when it should be winding down.

A 20-minute walk after dinner genuinely helps here — not because of the exercise itself, but because it lowers evening cortisol measurably. So does a consistent wind-down routine: same bedtime, same low-light environment, no difficult conversations or emails after 9pm.

Your Body Temperature Regulation Worsens

Deep sleep requires your core body temperature to drop by about one degree. After 40, this thermoregulatory system becomes less efficient. Waking up too hot in the night — especially between 2am and 4am — is a classic sign. Women approaching perimenopause experience this acutely as night sweats, but men aren't immune either.

Solutions that actually work: keep your bedroom cool (16–18°C is the evidence-backed sweet spot), use a lighter duvet than you think you need, and if you're still struggling, a cooling mattress pad makes a real difference.

The Pillow Problem Nobody Talks About

After 40, muscle tension in the neck and shoulders tends to increase — often from desk work, stress, or simply years of less-than-perfect posture. Sleeping on a pillow that doesn't properly support your neck keeps those muscles engaged all night, preventing the deep physical relaxation that restorative sleep requires.

I switched to an ergonomic contoured pillow a year ago and noticed a difference within a week. Less of a gimmick than most "sleep products," and the reviews from people over 40 are consistently good.

The 3am Wake-Up Is Its Own Problem

If you're regularly waking at 3am and can't get back to sleep, this is usually a blood sugar issue combined with elevated cortisol. Your blood sugar drops in the early hours, triggering a small cortisol spike to raise it — and that spike wakes you up. A small protein snack before bed (a handful of nuts, not a biscuit) can prevent the drop. So can cutting alcohol: it fragments sleep in the second half of the night even when it initially helps you fall asleep.

What to Actually Try First

If you take nothing else from this, start with these three changes:

  • Dim your lights and screens by 9pm every night — consistency matters more than perfection
  • Keep your bedroom at 17°C or cooler — most people sleep warmer than they should
  • Get up at the same time every morning, even on weekends — this anchors your circadian rhythm more than any other single habit

Sleep after 40 requires more intentionality than it did at 25. But it's absolutely fixable — and when you get it right, the improvement in energy, mood, and mental clarity is immediate and significant.
